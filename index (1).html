Research Sketch

Scaffolded vs. Direct AI Explanations: Effects on Conceptual Retention and Metacognitive Awareness in AI-Assisted Learning

Draft for discussion — early-stage proposal

Motivation

LLM-based tools generate fluent explanations that help students complete immediate tasks. However, in teaching AI and Python to 300+ students, I observed a recurring pattern: students could follow or reuse AI-generated explanations in the moment, but later struggled to explain the underlying concepts independently. The AI output was useful, but it sometimes bypassed the cognitive effort needed for durable understanding.

This connects with research on digital and AI interfaces that support human cognitive abilities. In particular, Prompto's work on cognitive-state-aware prompting suggests that the timing and form of an intervention shapes whether users are receptive to cognitive support. I am interested in applying this idea to AI-assisted learning: not only whether AI explains well, but whether the interface design helps learners engage in the cognitive work required to learn.

Research Question

When students use AI tools for learning, how does the format of AI-generated feedback — direct explanation vs. scaffolded self-evaluation prompt — affect conceptual retention and metacognitive awareness?

Proposed Study

A small exploratory user study comparing three AI explanation interfaces for learning programming or applied AI concepts:

  1. Direct explanation: the system gives a complete answer/explanation immediately.
  2. Socratic scaffolding: the system asks guided questions before revealing the explanation.
  3. Structured self-evaluation template: the learner first predicts, explains, and rates confidence before receiving feedback.

Participants would complete short learning tasks (Python, ML concepts, or debugging) with one interface condition, then complete immediate and delayed assessments.

Measures

Conceptual Retention Immediate post-test and delayed post-test
Metacognitive Accuracy Calibration between predicted and actual performance
Learning Process Interaction logs, time-on-task, revisions, help-seeking
User Experience Perceived effort, usefulness, frustration, and trust

Expected Contribution

This project would examine whether AI explanations should be designed as direct answers or as cognitive scaffolds. A possible contribution is an interface design pattern for LLM-based learning tools that balances answer quality with learner reflection, self-evaluation, and durable understanding.

What I Bring

Hands-on prototyping (Python, LLM engineering, interface implementation), practical learning-context experience from 300+ students, and applied AI system-building. I am motivated to learn rigorous HCI research methods through this project.
